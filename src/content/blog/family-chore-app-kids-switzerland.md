Every parent in Switzerland has had the same conversation. The dishwasher needs emptying. The bins need taking out. The school bag is still in the hallway. You mentioned it yesterday, and the day before. And yet here you are again.

Getting children to do household tasks consistently is less about willpower and more about systems. A family chore app for kids makes the invisible visible: who is responsible for what, whether it has been done, and what is coming up next.

## Why Paper Chore Charts Stop Working

Physical chore charts — the laminated poster on the fridge, the whiteboard in the kitchen — work brilliantly for about two weeks. Then the marker goes missing, someone forgets to tick their box, the chart falls behind the fridge, and the system quietly dies.

The problem is not the concept. It is the medium. A paper chart requires manual upkeep, is invisible outside the kitchen, and offers no notifications. When a child is upstairs and a parent is in the car, the chart does nothing.

A digital chore app solves these friction points: tasks repeat automatically on schedule, every family member sees their responsibilities on any device, and completions are logged without a pen in sight.

## What Makes a Good Family Chore App for Kids

Not every task management app works well for children. Most productivity apps are designed for adults managing work projects — not for a seven-year-old who needs to feed the cat. Here is what actually matters:

**Simple, age-appropriate interface.** Children should be able to open the app, find their tasks, and mark them done without a tutorial. If the interface requires navigating nested menus, it will not last the week. The fewer taps between opening and completing, the better.

**Parent-assigned, child-visible tasks.** The parent sets up what needs to happen and when. The child sees their own list — not the whole family's admin. This separation matters: a child does not need to see the household budget reminders alongside "take the rubbish out."

**Recurring task support.** Chores are by nature repetitive. Daily tasks (make the bed, put school bag by the door) and weekly tasks (vacuum bedroom, help with shopping) should reset automatically. Manual re-entry every week guarantees the system will be abandoned.

**Works across the whole family, not just the children.** Chore apps that only target children create a double-standard that children notice. An app where parents also have tasks — and complete them — models the behaviour you are trying to teach.

**Privacy for children's data.** Because child profiles contain information about a minor — their name, their schedule, their habits — the data privacy standards should be higher, not lower. The app should not monetise child profile data through advertising or third-party sharing.

## Standalone Chore App or Integrated Family Organizer?

This is the key decision for most families. Purpose-built chore apps (OurHome, Chore Monster, Homey) focus entirely on task assignment and gamified completion. Integrated family organizers (like Eazy.Family) include chores as one module within a broader system that also covers the shared calendar, shopping lists, and family messaging.

**The case for standalone chore apps:** They tend to have more polished child-facing UX, with sticker rewards, point systems, and visual progress bars that appeal to younger children. If getting children engaged in the chore system is the primary challenge, a dedicated app can help.

**The case for an integrated family organizer:** Most Swiss families already need to manage a shared calendar, grocery lists, and family communication. Adding a separate chore app means yet another login, another subscription, and another app to check. When tasks are part of the same system as the calendar, parents can see at a glance what a child has coming up — chores alongside homework, sports, and appointments — without switching apps.
