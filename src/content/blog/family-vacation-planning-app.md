Planning a family holiday should be exciting. In practice, it often turns into a project management exercise: coordinating school holiday dates across different cantons, aligning work leave, booking the right accommodation before it fills up, and remembering who agreed to pack what. By the time the car is loaded and everyone is actually on their way, at least one person has usually been driven slightly mad by the logistics.

A good family vacation planning app does not eliminate the complexity of family travel. It does make the coordination bearable — and keeps everyone working from the same information.

## Why Family Holidays Are Harder to Plan Than They Look

The challenge with family travel is not any single task. It is the combination: multiple calendars, varying school break schedules, the need to communicate decisions to everyone, and the constant stream of small items that need tracking.

In Switzerland, school holiday dates differ by canton. A family in Zurich and a grandparent in Geneva are often looking at different school break windows. Families with children in bilingual schools or with relatives across the border in Germany, Austria, or France deal with even more schedule fragmentation. Getting everyone aligned on a two-week window in July requires more than a quick conversation.

Add packing lists, travel documents, activity planning, and the inevitable last-minute changes, and it becomes clear why family holiday prep tends to fall entirely on one person's shoulders.

## What a Family Vacation Planning App Should Actually Do

Not every app that calls itself a travel planner is suited to the specific chaos of family trips. Here is what genuinely useful features look like in practice.

**Shared calendar with holiday visibility.** The foundation of any trip is agreeing on dates. A family app with a shared calendar lets every adult see who is free, when school breaks fall, and what commitments are already locked in — before any booking is made.

**Shared to-do and packing lists.** "Did you pack the kids' sun cream?" is the kind of question that could be eliminated by a shared packing list everyone can check off in real time. The same applies to pre-trip tasks: booking transfers, renewing passports, confirming activity reservations.

**In-app messaging tied to the trip.** When a parent messages "the ferry departs at 8, not 9" that message should not get lost in a family WhatsApp thread. A purpose-built family app keeps trip-related communication in context, so the information is findable when it matters.

**Easy access for kids.** Children old enough to pack their own bags can be involved in the planning process through a child profile. Seeing their own packing list and countdown gives kids agency without exposing them to every logistical detail.

**Works offline.** Mountain hiking, ferry crossings, and remote campsites all come with unreliable mobile connections. The app should still be usable when there is no signal.

## How Existing Apps Handle Family Travel

Most general travel apps — TripIt, Google Trips, TravelMapper — are designed for solo or business travel. They handle flight confirmations and itineraries well, but they do not integrate with a family calendar, shared task lists, or family messaging. You end up managing the travel app alongside the family organizer, which defeats the purpose.

Cozi includes shared to-do lists and a family calendar, which makes it reasonable for holiday coordination, but the free tier is ad-supported and multilingual support is limited — a real drawback for Swiss families.

FamilyWall covers similar ground with a more social-media-style interface. It works for some families, but the feed format can obscure time-sensitive information like packing deadlines or departure reminders.

## A Simple System for Smoother Trips

You do not need to overengineer holiday planning. A few habits make a big difference when they are supported by the right tool:

Start a trip checklist six weeks out. Passports, insurance documents, accommodation confirmations, and any required visas all have lead times. A shared checklist visible to both parents means nothing slips through.

Use the calendar to work backwards from departure. If you leave on a Saturday, when does the car need to be packed? When are the kids finishing school? When is the last day to buy anything you are missing? Blocking these milestones in a shared calendar makes the run-up to departure much calmer.

Create a trip-specific packing list. Generic lists exist, but a list for this particular trip — hiking in the Alps versus beach in Italy — and for each family member keeps preparation efficient and reduces the "I thought you packed it" conversations.
